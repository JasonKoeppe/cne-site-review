What the job involves

More than an outlet — and that's the point

A hot tub circuit is a dedicated 50-or-60-amp, 220-volt run with its own disconnect within sight of the water and GFCI protection. Getting each piece right is what keeps the tub safe, the warranty valid, and the inspector happy.

The dedicated circuit

Properly sized wire from your panel to the tub — trenched, conduited, or routed through the structure, done clean.

Spa panel & disconnect

The outdoor spa panel (disconnect) your tub's manual requires, mounted where code puts it — so anyone can kill power fast.

GFCI protection

The piece that makes water and electricity coexist safely. Non-negotiable, and wired so it doesn't nuisance-trip every weekend.

Capacity first: a 50-amp tub is a big new load. If your panel's already full, we'll tell you before you're committed — and if you need a panel upgrade anyway, the tub circuit gets while-we're-there pricing on the same visit.

Hot tub & pool FAQ

What buyers ask before delivery day

The dealer said "you just need an electrician" — need what, exactly?

A dedicated 220-volt circuit, an outdoor disconnect (spa panel) within sight of the tub, and GFCI protection — sized to your specific model. Send us the model and we'll translate the manual into a quote.

Can I plug a hot tub into a regular outlet?

Some "plug-and-play" tubs run on a standard 120-volt GFCI outlet at reduced heating speed. Most full-size tubs need the 220-volt circuit. If you're deciding between models, call — the wiring cost difference is worth knowing before you buy.

How far ahead should I schedule?

Call when you order the tub. Most hookups schedule within 1–2 weeks, and doing the wiring before delivery means you're soaking the week the tub arrives instead of staring at a cold shell.

What about pools?

Pumps, heaters, salt systems, lighting, bonding — pool equipment wiring is its own discipline with its own code chapter, and we do it. Same process: photos and equipment list first, written quote before work.

Will this need a permit?

Frequently yes — new outdoor circuits are permitted, inspected work in much of our service area. We handle the paperwork where it's required and build the inspection into the schedule, so it's not your problem.
